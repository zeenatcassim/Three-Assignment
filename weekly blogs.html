Week One

I installed GitHub last year for a project and have used it a few times to share projects thus I am a bit familiar on how to use some features.

My goal for this course is to make easy to read and understandable websites. Some websites just have an overwhelming amount of information on it, displayed through complicated formats or structures. Which makes it hard to read and understand how the website functions and where to find what you are looking for. Most websites organize information into many different categories and as such, they have a table of contents to display this information.

However, for a first-time user to always refer to the homepage to find the table of contents to then click on a specific topic they were looking for, is tedious. To combat this problem, I have noticed that websites display the table of contents pop up on all pages. This does make the user experience or flow smoother. I also have realized that when surfing the internet or looking through websites, users have the expectation that the next page of information, related to the one they are reading, will show up next for them to access.

I think it is quite interesting how the human brain stores information. In the reading, As We May Think, Bush states that humans think through pathways. They have one thought that leads to another, and this is how they remember information stored in their minds. Whereas machines have to browse through the information stored in them to bring up a topic. The reading then touches on the idea of selection by association. This stands out to me because nowadays when users use search engines, they can filter their ideas by selecting and deselecting topics. Or even just browse through ideas to find something that interests them. This in a way accommodates for the way the human brain thinks. Because then, the user clicks on the topic that interests them which will then lead them to having another thought and they may or may not, find something on the website that corresponds to that next thought.

Reference - Bush, V., 1945. As we may think. The atlantic monthly, 176(1), pp.101-108.

Week Two

I have prior experience with html. I am familiar with the simple methods of coding html, such as headings, titles and paragraphs. However, during this past weeks, the work has come off as overwhelming at times but I am trying to take my time to understand the code and actually write it out so that I can understand it better. I have read both the readings and from both of them, I have realised that coding websites is not only about displaying information.

Websites need to be functional, accessible and easy on the eyes. Performance is hugely important too but this is also hugely linked to the functionality of the website. The website can only perform well if it functions well. Hierarchy is important, we need to distribute information in such a way that makes it easy for readers to read and understand. However, the way it looks is very important. Websites need to have the right background colours, font sizes, font types and decorative elements. It should not look overly pretty but it should not look ugly. It must look good enough for readers willing to stay and read its contents.

In my opinion, these two factors work together. When a site looks good, readers stay and browse through it but readers also need to, unconsciously or consciously, be aware of the information hierarchy. By having the main heading appear larger than the section headings, makes the reader aware that these section headings are just smaller topics of the main heading. Thus, why it is important to separate information into their own containers. It leans more into the information hierarchy. Websites also need to work well in order to be successful. The links, the buttons and the functionality need to respond to the reader’s action. If none of them work, the reader will immediately log off the website because they are not getting what they came for. The functionality part of a website works because of the html code. In conclusion, the designer needs to place information into containers and then display these in a hierarchy while also making their code work with the aim that the functionality of their code will make the website perform well.

The concept of convention from the reading ties into UI and UX. In the lecture video, it can be surmised that the door had a poor design because users did not like how it worked. That specific door handle has the convention of pulling it to open the door. In this case, this convention is completely changed because this door handle requires users to push it to open the door. Since the convention is different, users are unfamiliar with it and as a result, don’t like it. Conventions are practices that are widely accepted and used in daily life. In the reading, it is stated that when a new practice is started, it shocks the audience and its medium. If others adopt this practice, then it becomes convention (Mailloux 1983: 399). In the case of the door handle, more than shock. Users despise the door handle and due to this, it can’t become a convention.

With this idea applied to websites and their Ui/Ux. It is safe to assume that new practices can be started with them but as long as the reader doesn’t hate it. Do not take existing conventions and change their function. However, websites need to maintain their standard conventions of Ui and Ux because readers are used to certain design structures of websites. You can not code a paragraph to look like a heading, the paragraph’s convention is that its text is smaller than the heading to show that it is giving more insight to it. If you change this convention, it doesn’t make sense and as a result, readers are going to despise it. If you do want to start a new convention, don’t change its function, rather expand more on it. For example, you can change the paragraph’s font, text color, background color or have the reader click on the heading to read the paragraph. In this case, the function doesn’t change, and readers can still understand it. I think it's good that we can access lectures that offer us different perspectives on the content. Watching them really helps deepen my understanding of the work. It broadens your perspective and lets you looks at concepts or problems from different viewpoints and this really helps when it comes to problem solving.

How do the two readings relate and how do they impact your thinking about web dev as a South African

The readings relate to each other because they both speak on the topics of geographic information. The first reading, Towards a study of information geographies: (im)mutable augmentations and a mapping of the geographies of information, introduces the idea that information has always had geography and that with time, devices that can transport information without significant change to its characteristics and meaning, have appeared. These devices are called immutable mobiles and due to these, information was able to be transferred and transported around easily. The immutable devices made spreading information easy and as a result, more information specific to places became known to more people. These are called (im)mutable augmentations because they act as containers of information for a geographic place that people can access. Thus, these geographic distributions of info becomes a means for political contestation to occur.

The second reading, Digital Hegemonies: The Localness of Search Engine Results, also speaks about (im)mutable augmentations but rather calls them digital augmentations of places. This reading touches on troubling concepts such as geographic information being produced and then becomes a producer of power relations. The search engine which is designed to help users search for specific ideas, topics, concepts, etc on the internet then becomes a deciding factor in which information should be presented to whom. These readings leads one to think that web development is not as simple as coding websites and apps to share and distribute information to the public. The way in which information is shared and the sources from which it comes from is very important. Many people blindly trust and follow the internet without stopping to think or question the legitimacy of the information they are reading. Due to this, it can be said that manipulating information, about a geographical place or anything else even, on the internet is very easy because people trust the internet.

References: Ballatore, A., Graham, M. and Sen, S., 2017. Digital hegemonies: the localness of search engine results. Annals of the American Association of Geographers, 107(5), pp.1194-1215.

Graham, M., De Sabbata, S. and Zook, M.A., 2015. Towards a study of information geographies:(im) mutable augmentations and a mapping of the geographies of information. Geo: Geography and environment, 2(1), pp.88-105.

(2017), Theorizing Digital Divides, Routledge, pp. 21-34. In their text “Social capital and the three levels of digital divide”, Ragnedda and Ruiu investigate and discuss how social capital can influence digital divide and the relation social capital has with the three levels of digital divide before pointing out digital capital stands on its own but is ultimately connected to other forms of capital. “social capital is functional to the maintenance and reproduction of the existing social structure (see also Field, 2008) both offline and online.” Ragnedda and Ruiu begin their paper with a discussion on what social capital is before they conclude that social capital produces and reproduces inequality in the online sphere.

Social capital is likened to a credential because it allows access of resources to group members that a group has permission to use. Due to this, existing social capital of internet users can promote the existence of virtual communities that aim to produce benefits for its members. As a result, maintaining inequalities between people but in the online sphere. “Indeed, one of the missions of the Internet is to connect people, by creating new and reinforcing existing social networks. The Internet may also support users’ in increasing their social capital while gaining benefits in economic, personal and cultural terms. However, in order to do this, citizens are supposed not only to access to Internet, bridging the first level of digital divide, but to have particular skills (technical, social, critical, strategic and creative) and motivation in using the Internet.”

There are levels of digital divide between those who can or cannot access the internet, those who want to and don’t want to use the internet or see no purpose in using it and lastly those who want to and don’t want to invest their time in building connections with people in the online sphere or learning how to access and spread valuable information, again in the online sphere. An important factor such as user’s backgrounds is mentioned in the reading because a user’s background has an influence on whether they have access to the internet, if whether they are well versed in using the internet and what information can they search for, process or even share on social media while using the internet. This all leads into how well a user can use the internet to their benefit in the social, economic or even cultural spectrum.

The Road to Technocolonialism

This reading touches on the concept of AI and its capabilities, benefits and also its drawbacks before stating that using AI can lead to techno colonialism. Artificial intelligence is powerful in the sense that it affects economies and power relations. It also has influence over military operations. The benefit of using artificial intelligence is that it can contribute greatly to global productivity.

However, the problem begins with governments and/or countries not having the infrastructure to bring artificial intelligence into various operations. Only certain countries, like the USA and China have thoroughly developed AI and developing countries have to turn to China or USA for AI access. In turn, superpowers like China and the USA request for economic and political gain. They receive data of citizens from the developing countries and also get more geopolitical influence over them.

A way to overcome this problem would be for developing countries to invest more in making their own infrastructure for AI access in their countries. If developing countries become independent, they won’t need to rely on a third party to let them access AI and its systems. They also won’t be forced to abide by political decisions of the superpowers it is reliant on. However, building up and maintaining the infrastructure for AI can take a long time and before developing countries can make their own, they have to rely on a superpower to give it to them, leaving them in a techno-colonial relationship with their superpower. A techno-colonial relationship refers to one party receiving political and economic gain and the other, technological access.

This might seem as a reasonable trade but it is not. Countries are trading their citizen’s data and giving their superpower more geopolitical influence over the people from their country. This indirectly gives the superpower a form of control over the developing country, they make them reliant on them and have an opportunity to exploit this relationship, by making them comply with the political decisions they enforce irrespective of it being ethically correct or not.

Reference – Verdi, G. 2020. Institute for Internet & the Just Society. The Road to Technocolonialism. Online. https://www.internetjustsociety.org/the-road-to-techno-colonialism . Date Accessed: 2/06/2023.

The benefits of responsive design is that it makes interaction with the website easy for the user/reader. When users interact with a website, they need to make decisions and these decisions need to be expressed clearly on the website’s interface (Geyser 2023). Based on this, it is safe to deduce that this is where responsive design occurs. Points of interaction need to be designed in a way “that leads and interests your user” (Geyser 2023), this is what responsive design is.

But responsive design can be challenging. Since it is supposed to make interaction easier and interesting, is it supposed to be creative? Having a creative design does make users interested but do users prefer to interact with designs they are used to or are they open to seeing and interacting with new designs?

In my website, while designing my wireframes. How we code matters and close reading:

Programming is always regarded as being neutral. It is regarded as just being a bunch of letters and numbers that a programmer can put into a software program and it will run a function. This is true and not true at the same time. Words mean something and thus, programming languages do have meaning (Geyser 2023). They output something, words and numbers or they run an operation.

Programming has become such an important factor in running daily operations. It has made countless programs and software that has become essential for technology use. While it has proved to have many benefits and advantages, there are still drawbacks in this industry.

One of the major drawbacks is how it is being used in global politics. In a close reading of Algorithmic Colonization of Africa, Abeba Birhane talks about the algorithmic colonization in Africa and how political, economic and ideological domination occurs with a technological justification.

“Common to both traditional and algorithmic colonialism is the desire to dominate, monitor, and influence social, political, and cultural discourse through the control of core communication and infrastructure mediums”

Western technology has grown and developed their AI technology. Using this, they have developed much of their operations with AI and introduced it into the South. This has been done to the point that daily operations cannot be performed without the integration of AI. This has resulted in making workflow more efficient but the dependence of technological infrastructures on Western powers leaves the South at a disadvantage. Not only that but through social communication mediums, the people of the South are at risk of getting influencing into agreeing with any political, social and cultural discourse that western powers expose them to.
